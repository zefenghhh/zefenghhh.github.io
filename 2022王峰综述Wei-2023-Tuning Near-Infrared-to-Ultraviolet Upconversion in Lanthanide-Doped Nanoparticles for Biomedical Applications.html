  1. Introduction

    Ultraviolet (UV) light is a powerful tool for biomedical,[1] envi- ronmental,[2] and industrial applications.[3] In the biomedical field, UV light can be used as a light source to trigger photoac- tivatable systems for applications in biosensing,[4] bioimaging,[5] photodynamic therapy (PDT),[6] and drug release.[7] In this regard, many photoactivatable systems have been developed such as photocage structures and DNA nanodevices.[8] How- ever, UV light is harmful to biological tissue and also has a poor tissue penetration depth, which impedes its in vivo application and clinical use.   2. Strategies for NIR-to-UV Upconversion

    To realize NIR-to-UV upconversion emission, a sensitizer with a sufficient absorption cross-section in the NIR region is essen- tial. The selection of appropriate sensitizer co-doped along with the activator through an efficient energy transfer process could realize strong UV upconversion emissions under different excitation wavelengths (Figure 1). For example, Yb3 is an ideal sensitizer for 980 nm excitation because of its larger absorption cross-section at around 980 nm than other lanthanide ions due to the 2F7/2  2F5/2 transition.[23] Additionally, the 2F5/2  2F7/2 transition of Yb3 well matches electronic transitions in typical upconverting activators (e.g., Er3, Tm3, and Ho3) that facili- tates energy transfer upconversion (ETU).[24] Nd3 is particularly suitable for use as a sensitizer to absorb shorter-wavelength laser due to its multiple NIR excitation bands at 730, 808, and 865 nm, corresponding to transitions from the 4I9/2 state to the 4F7/2, 4F5/2, and 4F3/2 states, respectively. Nevertheless, the emis- sion of activators could be quenched when utilizing Nd3 as a sensitizer because of the deleterious cross-relaxation between activators and Nd3.       Core–shell nanostructural engineering is a powerful com- plementary to compositional design for constructing 980 nm- to-UV upconversion. Notably, designing and synthesizing core–shell nanostructured UCNPs based on tetrafluoride hosts (e.g., LiLnF4, NaLnF4, and KLnF4; Ln  Y, Gd, Lu) have lots of advantages such as standard preparation protocol,[14] controllable morphology,[16,27b] tunable size,[12,43] and enhanced upconversion emission.[44] In a representative example, our group designed a core–shell–shell nanostructure composed of NaYF4@NaYbF4:Tm@NaYF4 for confining energy migration in Yb3 ions to emit strong UV light at 290 nm under 980 nm excitation (Figure 2d).[45] By controlling the thickness of the NaYbF4:Tm layer, we demonstrated that the confinement of energy migration in a prescribed space could suppress energy reduction and improve the local density of excitation energy (Figure 2e). At an optimal shell thickness of 3 nm, the Tm3 emission at 290 nm was enhanced by over 45-fold compared to nanoparticles doped low concentration of Yb (19%). Com- pared to the synthesis of special host materials for intensive UV emission, the core/shell design enhances optical tunability in a given nanoparticle composition.

      Recently, Zhou and co-workers designed a modified core–shell structure composed of LiYbF4:Tm (1 mol%)–LiYbF4@LiYF4 to emit strong UV light at 289 (1I6/3H6), 347 (1I6/3F4), and 362 nm (1D2/3H6) through spatial confinement of the lanthanide activa- tors inside LiYbF4 sensitizing lattice.[46] By tuning the Yb3 con- centration in the LiYbF4 sensitizing margin region, they demon- strated that a higher Yb3 sensitizer concentration could boost

      the localized energy density to activate the multiphoton upcon- version process, realizing a high-efficiency 980 nm-to-UV upcon- version process. They revealed that the optimal separation of the LiYbF4 sensitizing margin region and Tm3 ions is 2 nm by con- trolling the thickness of the LiYbF4 region.

      UV upconversion emission can also be enhanced by con- trolling excitation parameters. Recently, Romanowski and co- workers adopted a strategy to boost upconverted UV emissions by modulating excitation pulse width.[47] They demonstrated that UV upconversion emission of NaYF4:Yb/Tm (30/0.5%) nano- particles was gradually enhanced following pulse width increase from 10 s to 2 ms, as shown in Figure 2f.     2. UV Emission by 808 nm Excitation


      Excitation at 808 nm is advantageous over 980 nm for bio- medical applications. Since biological tissues display maximum transparency in the 700–900 nm range,[48] 808 nm excitation can overcome the overheating issues associated with water absorption and improves the penetration depth in deep-tissue applications. Organic dye molecules, such as indocyanine green (ICG) and IR-806 with high absorptivity at 808 nm, have been attached to the surface of NaGdF4 or NaYF4-based UCNPs for UV emission by 808 nm excitation.[49] However, using organic dye to sensitize UCNPs suffer from limitations in biological applications due to their low biostability, poor photostability, and potential safety issues. Therefore, the research is mainly directed to Nd-sensitized systems.[50]

      Conventionally, the doping concentration of Nd3 in upcon-

      version materials should be kept at a very low level to minimize the quenching effect of Nd3 and upconverting activators (i.e., Er3, Tm3, and Ho3).[51] To break through this predicament, core–shell nanostructure has been widely used to enhance upconversion emission by spatially confining upconverting activators and Nd3 sensitizers in different layers of the host materials.[50] In a later example, Yao and co-workers proposed a meaningful strategy to further enhance upconversion emis- sion by introducing a transition layer into the core–shell struc- ture.[52] The transition layer severed as a quenching-shield layer that suppressed energy back-transfer from activators to the Nd3 ions (Figure 3a). As a result, an unusually high concentra- tion of Nd3 ions (90%) can be doped in the sandwich structure to achieve highly efficient upconversion emissions of Tm3, Ho3, and Er3 ions under 808 nm excitation (Figure 3b).

Second, doping Yb3 ions is critical for mediating the energy transfer from Er3 ions to Tm3 ions, in which excited energy is further amassed. Third, constructing the core–shell–shell nanostructure is a necessity for eliminating undesired energy exchange interactions among the Yb3, Tm3, Ce3, and Er3 ions. This work largely expands the scope of Er3-sensitized upconversion.


  3. NIR-to-UV Upconversion for Bioapplications

    Indirect UV generation through UCNPs offers many advan- tages for biomedical applications such as remote light delivery, localized emission, deep tissue penetration, and minimum tissue damage. In addition, UCNPs are highly amenable to surface modification, which provides tailorable functionalities for diverse biomedical applications ranging from diagnostic sensing and imaging to cancer therapy.


    1. Medical Diagnosis


      In the past few years, optical sensing has been widely used for medical diagnostics due to its capability to precisely and sensi- tively detect changes in microenvironments. UCNPs have been proven to be a powerful tool for sensing and imaging a variety of ionic and molecular species in combination with photoac- tivated systems, nano/microdevices, and photoacoustic (PA) techniques.


      1. Photoactivated Systems


        Biological processes are subjected to complex influences of H/ OH ions, trace metal elements, and small biomolecules, which can induce diseases by an abnormal concentration or fluctua- tion.[63] For example, the lack of essential trace metal elements can cause the reduction or complete loss of enzyme activities, thereby blocking the synthesis and metabolism of hormones, proteins, and vitamins. However, their excessive accumulation may cause detrimental effects.[64] Therefore, it is important to develop highly selective and sensitive sensors to detect pH, metal elements, and biomolecules in living organisms.[63b,65]

        Constructing photoactivated systems is a powerful strategy for sensitive biodetection. There are two basic requirements for constructing a photoactivated system: a photoactivated com- pound for probing the analyte and a light source for activating the compound.[66] The sensing activity of the photoactivated sensors is inert to target analytes before a photolysis reaction of photoactivated compounds induced by light irradiation, thereby

        enabling high spatial and temporal precisions compared to con- ventional sensing systems.[67] As photoactivated compounds are often responsive to UV/vis light that has poor penetration depth in tissues, its practical applications in biomedicine have met with limited success.[68] Therefore, the research has shifted to composite systems consisting of UCNPs as light transducers, which permit indirect photoactivation by NIR illumination.

        High precision sensing of pH was demonstrated by Zhao and co-workers using a DNA nanomachine, which was designed by rational engineering of the DNA motif with a light-responsive element (Figure 5a,b).[8c] In this system, the photocleavable (PC)-group contained complementary DNA (PC-strand) was utilized to block the i-motif strand (I-strand). Due to the Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET) effect between Cy5 in the PC- stand and the quencher in the I-strand, the fluorescence signal was low. By irradiation of upconverted UV light, the PC bond will be photolyzed, causing a decrease in the binding affinity between the PC-strand and I-strand. Consequently, the I-strand will fold into an intramolecular i-motif at low pH, resulting in the dissociation of the cleaved PC-strand and an increase in fluorescence due to the elimination of the FRET effect. The remotely controlled DNA nanodevice is promising for time- resolved activation of pH sensing and imaging in living cells.

        Li and co-workers realized optical detection of metal ions by conjugating a photocaged Zn2-specific DNAzyme to NaYF4:Yb/ Tm@NaYF4 UCNPs (Figure 5c).[69] In the DNAzyme–UCNP sensor system, a carboxyfluorescein (FAM) fluorophore and a Black Hole Quencher-1 (Q1) were functionalized to opposite ends of the substrate strand, with another Dabcyl quencher (Q2) on the enzyme strand. A 2-nitrobenzyl photocage group (PG) was modified at the ribonucleotide adenosine (rA) site to protect the substrate strand from metal-ion-induced cleavage, which could be photodissociated by the upconverted UV emis- sion at 365 nm. After PG was photodissociated, the presence of the target metal ion would lead to the breakage of the substrate strand into two product strands (P1 and P2) due to the reduced melting temperature. Consequently, the P2 strand with the fluorophore would be dehybridized from the enzyme strand, resulting in fluorescence turn-on. The sensor was used to detect Zn2 ions in zebrafish 3 dpf larvae, showing an enhance- ment of luminescence signal in response to endogenous Zn2 in the head region and the body region (Figure 5d).

        In another development, Li and co-workers reported a novel DNA nanodevice that combines a photoactivatable DNA probe with UCNPs for miRNA detection. (Figure 5e).[70] They intro- duced a PC bond into the hairpin loop of a molecular beacon to construct the UV light-responsive DNA probe (PBc), which was labeled with fluorophore Cy5 and black hole quencher (BHQ2) at the opposite ends. In their design, the fluorophore Cy5’s fluorescence was quenched by BHQ2 through FRET. When the probe was linked by a PC bond, the probe would temporarily inert to recognize and detect the target miRNA. After being irradiated by upconverted UV light, the PC bond would lyse and the cleaved PBc would target miRNA, resulting in strong fluorescence from Cy5. Compared to untreated Hela cells, the Hela cells treated with miR-21 inhibitor/mimic showed distinguishable decreased/increased fluorescence signals upon PBc-UN treatment and NIR light activation (Figure 5f).       3. Photoacoustic Imaging


        The physical basis of PA imaging is the conversion of electro- magnetic energy, such as optical or radio-frequency waves, into acoustic waves due to thermoelastic expansion. By measuring the time of arrival of the acoustic waves and knowing the speed of sound, an image can be reconstructed.[74] PA imaging com- bines optical excitation with ultrasound detection to achieve imaging beyond the limit of light diffusion, realizing high contrast and good spatial resolution.[75] Contrast agents are the core element of the PA imaging technique, which can improve

        image quality by changing the optical or ultrasonic perfor- mances of the surrounding tissue. UCNPs in PA imaging can function as contrast agents to generate acoustic waves or as light transducers to modulate other PA agents.

        In 2014, Tan and co-workers demonstrated that lanthanide- doped particles could produce PA contrasts due to non-radiative relaxations. By studying the 1530 nm IR emission properties of NaYF4:Yb/Er particles corresponding to the 4I13/2  4I15/2 transi- tion of Er3, they showed that energy loss through non-radiative relaxations could proportionally generate useful PA signals.[76] In 2016, the team further studied the size and shell effects on the PA properties of NaYF4:Yb/Er nanoparticles. The results showed that by increasing the particle size from 11 to 38 nm, the PA amplitude was enhanced due to the suppression of energy dissipation at the nanoparticle surface. Thus, the absorbed exci- tation energy was effectively transformed into PA signals by non-radiative relaxation within the lanthanide levels.[77] The PA signal amplitude was further increased by 10–30% by coating the NaYF4:Yb/Er nanoparticles (11, 18, 29, 38 nm) with a protec-

        tive shell of NaYF4 (18, 18, 11, 12 nm), which spatially separated lanthanide ions and surface quenchers. These studies substan- tiate the viability of UCNPs for PA imaging and raise the quest for further increasing PA productivity.

    2. Cancer Therapy


      Cancer has constantly threatened human lives. However, traditional cancer therapies (e.g., chemotherapy and radio-

      therapy) have shown limited therapeutic outcomes. It is thus imperative to develop new treatments for cancer therapy. Photosensitive materials based on UCNPs are promising for efficiently destructing tumors through controlled drug delivery and PDT.


          1. Drug Delivery


            Over the past decade, UCNPs-based drug delivery systems (DDSs) have been extensively explored for cancer therapy because UCNPs as light transducers can overcome the draw- backs of poor tissue penetration and inevitable living tissue damage by direct UV-light controlled drug release. In addition, UCNPs permit localized UV generation for controllable drug release with high spatial resolutions.

            The strategy of constructing photocaged systems is an ideal approach to effectively control drug release for cancer therapy.[8a,80] In a representative example, Xing and co-workers designed a nanostructure that adopts a mesoporous silica shell around NaYF4:YbTm@NaYF4 UCNPs to encapsulate antitumor drug doxorubicin.[8a] The UCNPs can convert the 980 nm light into UV emission to realize the photocleavage of the o-nitrobenzyl (NB) caged linker to control drug release. By con- structing this photocaged system, high-efficiency drug release was achieved in cancer cells, providing a new chance for drug release in living systems.

          2. Photodynamic Therapy


      PDT has attracted significant attention for the treatment of various cancer by using light to activate therapeutic agents locally.[86] PDT based on UCNPs has been widely used for cancer therapy because UCNPs enable the activation of pho- tosensitizers by a broadened spectrum of lights, in addition to serving as a platform for photosensitizer coupling and delivery.[87]

      Titanium dioxide (TiO2) can absorb UV light and generate reactive oxygen species (ROS). However, UV light has a lim- ited penetration depth in tissues, which limits its practical use in biomedicine. Combining UCNPs and TiO2 can solve this problem through NIR excitation. In a representative example, Lin and co-workers designed core/shell nanocomposites com- posed of TiO2-coated UCNP (UCNPs@TiO2) (Figure 9a).[88] In this nanocomposite, TiO2 nanoparticles can absorb the UV light converted by NaYF4:Yb/Tm@NaGdF4:Yb UCNPs and generate ROS to kill cancer cells and control tumor growth. Although the combination of UCNPs and TiO2 can be used to generate ROS and kill cancer cells, the low quantum efficiency of the UCNPs has limited its further application. Recently, Kim and co-workers reported a nanostructure composed of Au NR@ aTiO2@UCNPs,[89] in which Au core can enhance the upcon- version emission of the UCNPs by localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) for effective activation of TiO2 photosensi- tizers. In their work, the cancer cell viability through Au NR@ aTiO2@UCNPs nanocomposites treated was 28% compared to Au NR@aTiO2 (77.3%) and UCNP@aTiO2 (98.8%). The authors ascribed the results to efficient radical formation and LSPR- induced heat generation.

      Low ROS yield is one of the reasons hindering the clinical translation of PDT. Fenton reaction can produce abundant ROS through the chain reaction between two valence iron (Fe2) and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) under UV/vis irradiation,[90]

      which could be a solution to this problem. Shi and co-workers reported a nanocomposite of UCSRF for cancer therapy based on the Fenton reaction.[90b] The researchers employed a mesoporous silica shell (representing S in UCSRF) coated on NaYF4:Yb/Tm@NaYF4 UCNPs (representing UC in UCSRF) for Fe2 (Fenton reagent, representing F in UCSRF) loading and used Ru2 (representing R in UCSRF) complex to bind mitochondrial DNA (Figure 9b). By designing this nanocom- posite, the cancer cells’ mitochondrial DNA can be damaged by the ROS produced by the Fenton reaction under 980 nm irradiation, which was manifested by reductions in cancer cell viability in vitro and tumor volume in vivo. In 2019, Lin and co-workers constructed a yolk mesoporous nanoplatform by coating a mesoporous ZnFe2O4 shell on NaGdF4:Yb/Tm@ NaGdF4:Yb UCNPs for cancer therapy.[91] The yolk mesoporous nanoplatform, designed as Y-UCSZ, comprised a UCNP, a silica interlayer, and a mesoporous ZnFe2O4 shell. The UCNPs can efficiently convert NIR photons into UV light, which can activate ZnFe2O4 to generate ROS based on the Fenton reac- tion. The produced ROS can efficiently kill cancer cells and control tumor growth.

      Except for using the Fenton reaction to produce abundant ROS, exploring other oxidation radicals to promote PDT effi- cacy is also a promising strategy. For example, Bu and co- workers constructed a chlorine radical (·Cl) nano-generator composed of NaYF4:Yb/Tm (20/0.5%)@NaYF4@SiO2@Ag0/ AgCl@PEG (UCASP) for cancer therapy (Figure 9c).[92] Under 980 nm laser irradiation, the UCNPs emit UV/vis emission, which can be absorbed by the Ag0/AgCl heterostructure to transfer photogenic electrons to each other, thus effectively pro- ducing ·Cl of strong oxidation for cancer therapy. This strategy has a good effect on killing both normoxia and hypoxic tumors in vitro and in vivo.

      The PDT efficiency may be reduced by the poor spectral match of UCNPs and photosensitizers. Therefore, finding appropriate photosensitizers is extremely urgent for devel- oping PDT.[93] In 2017, Bu and co-workers reported a UCNP (NaYF4:Yb/Tm (20/0.5%)@NaGdF4)-SnWO4 nanohybrid system (USW) to enhance PDT efficiency, in which SnWO4 has a broad UV/vis absorption, as shown in Figure 9d,e.[94] It can make full use of the upconverted emission from UCNPs and maximally produce ROS for cancer therapy. Based on cancer cell viability assay in vitro and tumor volume measure- ment in vivo, the USW showed remarkable NIR-induced ROS productivity.


  4. Conclusion and Outlook

This review has described the recent development of NIR-to- UV light conversion through lanthanide-doped UCNPs and its utility in biomedicine. By judicious design of nanoparticle com- position coupled with core–shell nanostructural engineering, intense UV emissions are now readily accessible by NIR exci- tation of cost-effective diode lasers at 808, 980, and 1550 nm, respectively. These UCNPs as light transducers have enabled various biomedical detection and therapy techniques that rely on high-energy UV radiations for photoactivation or illumination.
